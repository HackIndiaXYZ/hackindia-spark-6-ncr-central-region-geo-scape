QGIS GEOSPATIAL ANALYSIS — STUDY AREA: HIMACHAL PRADESH, WESTERN HIMALAYAS

We conducted a comprehensive geospatial analysis of the landslide-prone sectors across the Mandi–Kullu watershed corridor using QGIS 3.x geoprocessing tools. The study area spans the Beas River valley and adjacent mountain slopes in Himachal Pradesh, a region classified as Seismic Zone IV–V with annual average rainfall exceeding 1,500mm. Below are the sequential raster layers we generated to build a multi-criteria Landslide Susceptibility Index (LSI) for optimal sensor node deployment.

01 — Digital Elevation Model (SRTM DEM)Source: NASA SRTM 30m

The foundation of all terrain analysis. This DEM raster was acquired from NASA's SRTM (Shuttle Radar Topography Mission) dataset at 1 arc-second (~30m) resolution. Elevation values range from 585m (valley floor, Beas River) to 6,473m (peak glacial ridges). Darker pixels represent deeper valleys and drainage corridors — the primary zones where water accumulates and slope failure initiates. This layer is the input for all subsequent derivative rasters.

02 — Contour Map (Elevation Isolines)Interval: 50m spacing

Generated using the GDAL Contour algorithm from the DEM layer. Each contour line represents a constant elevation interval, allowing us to visually identify steep terrain (closely-spaced lines) vs. flat plateaus (widely-spaced lines). Dense contour clustering in the southwest and northeast sectors directly correlates with high-gradient slopes where shear stress exceeds soil cohesion — the primary physical precondition for translational and rotational landslides.

03 — Hillshade Relief ModelAzimuth: 315° · Altitude: 45°

The hillshade raster simulates solar illumination across the terrain surface from a northwest light source at 45° altitude. This three-dimensional shaded relief highlights micro-topographic features invisible on raw DEM — such as existing landslide scarps, debris fans, escarpment edges, and ancient failure planes. Our team used this layer to visually validate identified risk zones before deploying hardware at the Alpha, Beta, and Gamma node positions.

04 — Aspect Map (Slope Orientation)Range: 0°–360° compass bearing

Aspect identifies the compass direction each slope faces. South-facing slopes (warm tones, 135°–225°) receive maximum solar radiation, leading to faster snowmelt, increased soil pore-water pressure, and consequently higher landslide susceptibility. North-facing slopes (cool tones, 315°–45°) retain moisture longer but experience freeze-thaw cyclical weakening. This directional analysis was weighted at 15% in our final Landslide Susceptibility Index (LSI) calculation.

05 — Slope Gradient MapMax gradient: 77° · Critical threshold: >35°

The single most important factor in landslide susceptibility. Calculated using the QGIS native slope algorithm from the SRTM DEM. Green zones (<25°) represent stable terrain. Yellow zones (25°–35°) are moderately susceptible. Red zones (>35°) are critically unstable — these are the exact sectors where our hardware nodes are deployed. Statistical analysis of the study area shows 68% of historical landslide events in this region occurred on slopes exceeding 35°. This layer carries the heaviest weight (30%) in our multi-criteria LSI model.

Multi-Criteria Analysis Summary

By stacking these 5 derivative raster layers (DEM → Contour → Hillshade → Aspect → Slope) in a weighted overlay model, we generated the final Landslide Susceptibility Index (LSI) heatmap shown above. The LSI directly informed our hardware deployment strategy — all three sensor nodes (Alpha, Beta, Gamma) are positioned in zones scoring ≥0.72 on the 0–1 susceptibility scale, ensuring maximum detection sensitivity while minimizing false-positive triggers from geologically stable terrain.
